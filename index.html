Metric Definitions

Definitions for all metrics calculated in this terminal. Click the button to toggle formula display.

Value at Risk (VaR)

The maximum expected loss over a given time horizon at a specified confidence level. A 95% 1-day VaR of 5% means there is a 5% chance of losing more than 5% in a single day based on historical returns.

VaRα = −Percentile(R, α)
e.g. 95% VaR = −5th percentile of daily returns
Conditional VaR / Expected Shortfall (CVaR / ES)

The average loss in the worst α% of scenarios — i.e. the expected loss given that the loss exceeds VaR. Always larger than VaR; considered a more complete tail risk measure.

CVaRα = E[R | R ≤ VaRα]
= Mean of all returns below the VaR threshold
Annualized Volatility

The standard deviation of log returns scaled to a one-year period. Uses 365 trading days as the annualization factor since crypto markets trade every day including weekends and holidays.

σann = σdaily × √(365 / horizon)
where σdaily = std(log returns)
Sharpe Ratio

Annualized return divided by annualized volatility. Measures return earned per unit of total risk. A Sharpe above 1 is generally considered good; above 2 is excellent. Assumes a risk-free rate of zero.

Sharpe = Rann / σann
Rann = mean(log returns) × (365 / horizon)
Sortino Ratio

Like the Sharpe ratio but only penalizes downside volatility (negative returns). A higher Sortino relative to Sharpe means the asset's volatility is skewed to the upside, which is desirable.

Sortino = Rann / σdownside
σdownside = std(negative returns only) × √365
Calmar Ratio

Annualized return divided by maximum drawdown. Measures how efficiently an asset recovers from its worst historical loss. Higher is better; values above 1 indicate strong risk-adjusted performance.

Calmar = Rann / |Max Drawdown|
Maximum Drawdown

The largest peak-to-trough decline in cumulative portfolio value over the full history. Represents the worst loss an investor could have experienced if they bought at the peak and sold at the trough.

MDD = mint[(Vt − Peakt) / Peakt]
Peakt = max(V0...Vt)
GARCH(1,1) Conditional Volatility

A time-varying volatility model that accounts for volatility clustering — the tendency for large moves to follow large moves. More responsive to recent market conditions than a simple rolling standard deviation.

σ²t = ω + α·ε²t−1 + β·σ²t−1
ω=1e−6, α=0.10, β=0.85
Daily vol = √σ²t · 100%
Ann. vol = √σ²t × √365 · 100%
Log Returns

The continuously compounded return between two prices. Preferred over simple returns for statistical analysis because they are time-additive and better approximate a normal distribution.

rt = ln(Pt / Pt−h)
where h = time horizon in days
Weighted VaR Contribution

Each token's individual VaR scaled by its portfolio weight. Indicates how much each position contributes to overall portfolio tail risk. Does not account for diversification effects between tokens.

Wtd VaRi = wi × VaRi
where wi = portfolio weight of token i
Pearson Correlation

Measures the linear relationship between two tokens' returns, ranging from −1 (perfect inverse) to +1 (perfect co-movement). Lower correlation between holdings improves diversification and reduces portfolio VaR.

ρ(X,Y) = Σ(X−X̄)(Y−Ȳ) / √[Σ(X−X̄)² · Σ(Y−Ȳ)²]
Liquidation Risk

Estimates the probability of a single-day return breaching the margin threshold given your leverage ratio, based on historical frequency. A higher leverage ratio lowers the return needed to trigger liquidation.

Threshold = −Margin% / Leverage
P(liq) = #{days r ≤ threshold} / #{total days}
Monte Carlo Simulation

Simulates thousands of possible future portfolio paths. Returns are drawn using a Cornish-Fisher adjusted distribution that incorporates the historical skewness and excess kurtosis of the portfolio, producing more realistic tail outcomes than a plain normal distribution.

z* = Cornish-Fisher(z, S, K)
rsim = μ + σ · z*
μ = mean(historical returns)
σ = std(historical returns)
Final P&L = Σ rsim over horizon days
Skewness

Measures the asymmetry of the return distribution. Negative skew (left tail) means large losses occur more often than a normal distribution would predict — common in crypto. Positive skew means large gains are more frequent than large losses.

S = [Σ(r − μ)³ / n] / σ³
Excess Kurtosis

Measures the heaviness of the tails relative to a normal distribution. Positive excess kurtosis (leptokurtic) means extreme moves happen more often than normal — a defining feature of crypto markets. A value of 0 means normal tails.

K = [Σ(r − μ)⁴ / n] / σ⁴ − 3
Cornish-Fisher Adjustment

A correction applied to standard normal quantiles to account for skewness and excess kurtosis. Used in both Diversified VaR and Monte Carlo simulation to produce more accurate tail estimates than assuming returns are normally distributed.

z* = z + (z²−1)·S/6 + (z³−3z)·K/24 − (2z³−5z)·S²/36
where z = standard normal quantile
Diversified VaR

A parametric VaR estimate that accounts for the correlation between holdings. When assets are not perfectly correlated, the portfolio's true risk is lower than the sum of individual VaRs. Also adjusted via Cornish-Fisher for skewness and fat tails.

σp = √(wᵀ Σ w)
Σij = ρij · σi · σj
Div. VaR = μp + z* · σp
Diversification Benefit

The reduction in VaR achieved by holding multiple assets rather than a single concentrated position. Calculated as the difference between the sum of weighted individual VaRs (undiversified) and the correlation-adjusted portfolio VaR (diversified). Higher is better.

Benefit = Σ(wᵢ · VaRᵢ) − Diversified VaRp
